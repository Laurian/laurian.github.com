‘for magic consists in this, the true naming of a thing’ — Ursula Le Guin, A Wizard of Earthsea

    Naming & Magic

    July 29, 2011 by Laurian Gridinoc

    “An im­por­tant con­sid­er­a­tion for the cog­ni­tive tie be­tween stake­hold­ers and a project is its name” says Dorian Taylor, and he argues that random cryptonyms are better suited for project code names.

    I agree with his reasons but I believe, especially after 7 years in a branding & interactive agency (Grapefruit, which I co-​founded in 1999), that you also need to assign meaning to such cryptonyms when employ them.

    Arbitrary names are just handles, assigning meaning to them is instrumental for that cog­ni­tive tie be­tween stake­hold­ers and a project. If you don’t assign a meaning, the stakeholders will, individually, and that can undermine the communication within the project.

    From his four requirements for such cryptonyms, I would reverse the third one. You need a sense of scope and sometime of sequence (next version of) in a project and the best way to control it is with the project name.

    Controlling language is controlling thought, and such cryptonyms are not far from Newspeak…

    Let’s take two separate (fictional) worlds:

    • The Newspeak term for the English language is Oldspeak. (Nineteen Eighty-​Four, George Orwell)
    • Magic on Earthsea is primarily verbal. Everything has a true name in the Old Speech, the language of the dragons. One who knows the true name of an object has power over it. A person also has a true name; for safety’s sake, he or she will only reveal it to those he or she trusts implicitly. A “use” name, which has no magical property, suffices for everyday purposes. (Earthsea hexalogy, Ursula Le Guin)

    We have there three languages:

    1. Old Speech (the language of the dragons): controls the named thing
    2. English (Oldspeak): mostly descriptive
    3. Newspeak: thought control on who uses the named thing

    Now when you name something, what do you aim for?

    I aim for magic.

    Instead of a cryptonyms with artificial meaning, I take the description of the (yet to be) named thing, and through etymological wordplay I seek the forgotten true name.
