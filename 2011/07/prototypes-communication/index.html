    Prototypes & Communication

    July 17, 2011 by Laurian Gridinoc

    In his Knight-​Mozilla Learning Lab “How to prototype and influence people” lecture, Aza Raskin said “to design is to inspire participation” and one of the problem in inspiring participation is the communication bandwidth.

    It is interesting to observe that the bandwidth expansion is a byproduct of reducing ambiguity.

    To make the abstract concrete you will require more symbols (therefore requiring more bandwidth), soon on that scale the sequential communication (the write-​up) is replaced by a parallel one (the mockups) which defines a visual language, and the elements of that language will gain meaning through their actual usage within the prototype (meaning which can be communicated later by proxy with the video).

    The most difficult part is to be aware (and not to forget in the process) that the design is a shared communication between the designer and a person and the technology is just the medium.

    Clarisse de Souza in “The Semiotic Engineering of Human-​Computer Interaction (Acting with Technology)” shows that software is an intellectual artefact and it has the following features:

    • it encodes a particular understanding or interpretation of a problem situation
    • it also encodes a particular set of solutions for the perceived problem situation
    • the encoding of both the problem situation and the corresponding solutions is fundamentally linguistic (i.e., based on a system of symbols — verbal, visual, aural, or other — that can be interpreted by consistent semantic rules)
    • the artefact’s ultimate purpose can only be completely achieved by its users if they can formulate it within the linguistic system in which the artefact is encoded.

    And that’s why you need to iterate.

    Aza said “you are iterating your solution as well as your understanding of the problem” and “treat your code as throw-​away, but be ready to refactor”. Indeed, it is more about iterating that linguistic encoding system and less about code.

    The final assignment for this learning lab is all about communicating design, communicating with all the stakeholders involved with the proposed artefact: users, developers, designers, clients, owners and technical support professionals.

    Now, what is the progress of my idea on that scale? The write-​up was submitted to the MoJo (Mozilla + Journalism) challenge and that’s how I was invited to participate in this Knight-​Mozilla Learning Lab.

    I’m currently iterating on a blackboard, away from the computer. The actual layout of the physical space represents the process: computer for research, post-​it notes on the wall for interaction options and their reasons, a reading chair next to some essential books (Edward Tufte, etc.) then the blackboard.

    My iterations are now in between the post-​it notes and the blackboard through the books. For now, I’m iterating on a monologue, trying to make sense of what I want to communicate; next week I’ll move to paper mockups as means to start a dialogue.
